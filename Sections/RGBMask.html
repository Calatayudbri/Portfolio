Ahuacatl and the
Chromatic Temple

Puzzle Platformer · Unreal Engine 5 · C++

An ancient Mesoamerican temple holds three colour channels, each guarded by a spirit. Don the masks of Chichiltic the Jaguar, Xoxoctic the Monkey, and Texotli the Serpent to hide a colour from the world and uncover hidden paths, platforms, and enemies. I was responsible for the core gameplay systems: the RGB mask mechanic and its post-process pipeline, the custom camera manager, the projectile pool, the player controller, and the full death-and-reload flow — all written in C++ with Blueprint-extensible hooks for the rest of the team.

Core Mechanic

The Three Spirits — RGB Mask System

✦ System Architecture ✦

The central system I designed for the jam. Each spirit grants the player a mask that hides one colour channel from the world — the key design challenge was making every coloured object in the level react instantly and reliably to the active mask, regardless of how many actors are in the scene or in what order they spawn. I solved it with a three-class architecture built around a World Subsystem that decouples every actor from the player completely.

No Mask — all colour channels visible — no spirit's power active
Chichiltic — red channel hidden
Xoxoctic — green channel hidden
Texotli — blue channel hidden

Three-Class Architecture

I split the system into three classes with a strict dependency direction. ARGBMaskCharacter owns the active mask state and exposes it via a multicast delegate OnMaskChanged — nothing else stores the state. UMaskVisibilitySubsystem is a World Subsystem that binds to that delegate and maintains a TSet of weak pointers to every registered component. UMaskVisibilityComponent is the actor-side piece: any object that reacts to the mask adds this component and configures its own visibility rules, with zero knowledge of the player.

The tricky part in a jam context was spawn-order safety — the subsystem can initialise before the PlayerController even exists. I handled this with a 100 ms retry timer that keeps trying to bind until the pawn is ready, so the system works regardless of level load order.

1

Input triggers mask toggle

A dedicated UInputAction per colour fires the controller's ToggleMask() — with a 0.5 s cooldown to prevent accidental double-taps.

2

Delayed commit + post-process

OnMaskChangeStarted fires immediately for UI. After MaskChangeDelay, ApplyMaskChange() commits the new state and swaps the PostProcessVolume materials.

3

Subsystem notifies all actors

ApplyMaskToAll() iterates a TWeakObjectPtr set, prunes stale entries, and calls ApplyMask() on each living component.

4

Per-actor visibility & FX

Each component checks its EMaskVisibilityMode (hide-in or show-only), sets actor hidden state, collision, and tick. A Niagara effect can fire once on transition or run persistently while the actor is hidden.

Post-Process per Spirit

On top of visibility, each mask needed to shift the whole screen's colour grade. I used a single unbound PostProcessVolume and drove its WeightedBlendables array directly from C++ — no separate Blueprint logic required. I implemented two modes so the team could choose the feel that worked best for the game.

Instant swap

The WeightedBlendables array is cleared and rebuilt with the chosen material in a single frame. No latency — best for fast puzzle solving.

Smooth blend

With bUseSmoothBlending, a 60 Hz timer drives PostProcessBlendAlpha from 0 → 1 over a configurable duration, cross-fading both materials simultaneously.

Designer-friendly

All four material slots are EditDefaultsOnly. Artists swap materials in the BP derived class — blend weight and duration are exposed too.

Mask Inventory

To keep progression gating simple for the jam, I used a TMap<EMaskType, bool> pre-reserved for the three spirits. The input handler checks this map before calling ToggleMask() — equipping a spirit you haven't found yet silently does nothing, so level designers can place mask pickups anywhere without touching the controller logic. Both AddMaskToInventory() and DeleteMask() are Blueprint-callable so the team could wire them to pickups without writing C++.

Camera

Custom Camera Manager

✦ Volume-based Navigation ✦

Rather than relying on Blueprint camera logic, I subclassed APlayerCameraManager directly to own the full top-down pipeline in C++. This gave me precise control over the update order — base transform, offset, clamp, interpolation, modifiers — and made it trivial to add volume-based zone transitions without any Blueprint event graphs.

Zone Volumes

Volume discovery

On the first UpdateViewTarget(), FindCameraVolumes() gathers all ACameraVolume actors and sorts them by priority descending — higher-priority rooms win when temple areas overlap.

Active zone detection

Every frame, GetActiveVolume() walks the sorted array and picks the first volume whose bounding box contains the player, falling back to index 0 if the player is between rooms.

Room change

HandleVolumeChange() calls ShowActors() on the previous volume (restoring original visibility states) and HideActors() on the new one — hiding obstructing temple geometry automatically per zone.

Smooth transition

A bIsTransitioning flag activates VInterpTo at CameraTransitionSpeed. Once within 1 unit of target, the flag clears and the camera switches to a faster follow rate.

Position Pipeline

1

Base transform from Unreal

Super::UpdateViewTargetInternal() computes the raw camera transform without any modifiers applied.

2

Apply camera offset

ApplyCameraOffset() pushes the camera back along either the player's forward or a fixed world direction, by CameraBackwardOffset, and up by CameraHeightOffset.

3

Clamp to temple room

Target X and Y are clamped to the active volume's camera bounds (volume bounds expanded by CameraMargin) with a small slack to avoid hard snapping at room edges.

4

Interp + modifiers

Position interps via VInterpTo, rotation optionally via RInterpTo, then shakes and other modifiers apply through the standard ApplyCameraModifiers() call.

Camera Shake Subsystem

I built a UCameraShakeSubsystem to centralise all shake calls across the codebase. Any system — traps, projectiles, events — calls one function with an optional distance falloff radius and the subsystem handles cooldown tracking, intensity scaling, and gamepad rumble via dynamic force feedback. No caller needs to know about PlayerCameraManager directly.

Player

Movement & Death Flow

✦ Control & Feedback ✦

I implemented the player controller handling both top-down movement modes and the complete death sequence. One design goal was keeping the level-reload logic out of C++ so the rest of the team could change it freely in Blueprint without recompiling.

Movement

Directional input

Rather than using world-space axes, I project the 2D input vector onto the camera's yaw rotation so WASD always moves relative to what the player sees — important because the camera can rotate between temple rooms.

Enhanced Input

I set up all bindings through Unreal's Enhanced Input system from the start, even for a jam. Having mask keys, pause, and movement as separate UInputAction assets let the team rebind anything from the editor without touching C++, and made it trivial to add a pause action late in the jam as a BlueprintImplementableEvent.

Death & Reload

1

Die()

The first thing Die() does is push a dedicated UInputComponent with priority 100,000 — high enough to block every other binding instantly. Movement stops, the camera fades to black, and the death widget is added to viewport at ZOrder 9999.

2

HandleDeathReload()

After a configurable delay I call URGBMaskGameInstance::LoadGame(), declared as a BlueprintImplementableEvent with no C++ body. That one decision meant the team could change which level to reload, add save-game logic, or go to a menu screen — all from Blueprint, no recompile needed.

3

Same-level fallback

If the reload stays on the same map (common during jam testing), the code also pops the input blocker, fades back in, removes the widget, and restores the movement mode — so the pawn is always left in a valid playable state.

Sound

Audio Integration

✦ Mechanical Feedback ✦

Audio integration was kept intentionally lightweight for the jam, but I made sure the C++ systems exposed the right hooks so sound designers could wire everything from Blueprint without needing to modify any of the core classes.

Camera Shake + Rumble

The UCameraShakeSubsystem I built pairs every shake call with optional gamepad force feedback. Both are driven by the same distance falloff parameters, so impact events feel consistent across screen and controller simultaneously.

Mask Switch Hooks

I exposed OnMaskChanged and OnMaskChangeStarted as Blueprint-assignable multicast delegates. The team used them to trigger mask-switch stingers and UI sound effects without writing or modifying any C++.

Death Event

OnPlayerDead is also a Blueprint-assignable delegate on the character. Wiring a death sound cue to it required no C++ knowledge from teammates — just an event binding in the BP derived class.
